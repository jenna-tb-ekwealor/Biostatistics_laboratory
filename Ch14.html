Population genetics

Chapter 14

Today’s investigation. Marine mammals provide a powerful system for studying gene flow across broad, dynamic seascapes. In this lab, we will explore how population genetic tools can uncover structure among breeding colonies of South American fur seals (Arctocephalus australis). Specifically, we will analyze microsatellite genotypes from multiple regions to test whether fur seal populations are genetically differentiated—and if so, how much.

Worked example

Imagine two populations of lizards living on neighboring islands: Island A and Island B. A single microsatellite locus has two alleles: A and a. You sample 10 lizards from each population and genotype them at this locus. Here are the results:

1. Count alleles

Each individual has 2 alleles. Count alleles in each population:

  • Island A: A = 10, a = 10
  • Island B: A = 16, a = 4

2. Calculate allele frequencies

  • Island A: freq(A) = 0.50, freq(a) = 0.50
  • Island B: freq(A) = 0.80, freq(a) = 0.20

3. Calculate HT and HS

  • HT = 1 – (0.65² + 0.35²) = 0.455
  • HS = (0.5 + 0.32) / 2 = 0.41

4. Calculate FST

FST is a standardized measure of genetic differentiation between populations:

FST = (HT – HS) / HT

FST = (0.455 – 0.41) / 0.455 ≈ 0.099

5. Interpret your result

FST ≈ 0.10 suggests modest genetic differentiation. Most variation is still within populations, but allele frequencies differ enough between islands to detect some structure.

6. AMOVA concept

AMOVA partitions genetic variance within individuals, among individuals within populations, and among populations. In this case, most variation is within populations, but a portion is explained by population structure—what FST captures.
